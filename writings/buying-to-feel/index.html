Buying to Feel: Emotional Consumerism Among Female University Students in Digital Spaces

Digital platforms turn stress and insecurity into shopping habits by wrapping consumption in the language of self-care.

Introduction

Contemporary social life is increasingly shaped by the blend of physical and digital spaces, as online platforms become embedded in daily routines rather than existing apart from everyday life. In this augmented landscape, a noticeable pattern has emerged: digital environments are increasingly designed in ways that subtly shape and direct human desire. Social media platforms embed consumer goods within emotional narratives, presenting commodities not just as objects to purchase but as tools for self-care, social participation, and lifestyle improvement. This phenomenon is especially significant among female university students, a demographic highly influenced by social media trends and social expectations. Between classes, during late-night study sessions, or in the privacy of dorm rooms, digital platforms often become spaces where emotional discomfort is managed and interpreted. In these contexts, consumer desire does not emerge separately from emotion. Instead, it is produced through the very ways platforms organize attention, visibility, and interaction.

Emotional consumerism on social media is an outcome of platform design and culture. Platforms use recommendation systems, algorithmic content, and validating comment cultures to transform feelings such as insecurity, academic pressure, and the desire for recognition into consumer practices in which buying is framed as relief, self-improvement, and belonging. By focusing on everyday scenes of platform use, this paper shows how emotional consumerism is cultivated through ordinary digital interactions and how, for female university students in particular, platform environments increasingly function as systems that bind feeling to spending. The following scenes trace how emotional consumerism is experienced, normalized, and reinforced across different digital environments.

Scene 1: The “Material Girls” Community

Among female university students, a visible community of what might be called “material girls” has emerged on Red Note, where users gather not only to share wish lists and purchases but also to collectively normalize shopping as an emotional practice. During a break between studying in the library, Red Note becomes a quick emotional detour. The screen is filled with the usual stream of polished outfit photos, anime news, and some game information, when one post interrupts the rhythm of scrolling. The post, by the user @蝴蝶IRA, is titled: “I want to buy, but I don’t want to wear them; I wish I could just one-click share my wardrobe…” The post reads like a confession. The user describes repeatedly buying clothes, plush toys, and accessories, only to leave many of them untouched after purchase. Buying is described as something she turns to when she feels bad, and something that has gradually become difficult to control.

The comment section underneath does not correct or discipline her. As the visible replies accumulate, what appears most strongly is recognition. Users respond in short, emotionally direct phrases: they admit doing the same thing, describe shopping as a way to comfort themselves, or suggest that as long as the spending is not debt-driven, it is acceptable. Other comments morally reframe the behavior: at least it is not smoking, drinking, or gambling; at least it helps someone get through life; at least wanting beautiful things means one still has desire to live. The interaction is striking because the discussion rarely turns to whether the items are practical or affordable. Instead, the comments stay focused on feeling: how it feels to want, how it feels to buy, and how it feels to briefly feel better afterward.

The post is not just an individual confession but an entry point into the community of “material girls.” The comment section becomes a space of validation where consumption desire is not treated as irrational excess but as an understandable emotional response to stress, anxiety, and instability. This normalization is further reinforced by platform infrastructure: Red Note’s official recommendation disclosure states that the platform uses browsing, clicking, searching, liking, saving, sharing, commenting, and posting behavior to generate personalized content, and that one of its main goals is to help users discover lifestyles and meet consumption needs. Once the user lingers on, interacts with, or saves posts like this one, the platform can continue surrounding them with similar emotional-consumer narratives, turning shopping from a temporary response into a socially and algorithmically sustained coping pattern. This pattern reveals how Red Note normalizes shopping as an emotionally meaningful practice. It becomes possible to observe how this logic is internalized and lived by individual users.

scene 2: Consumption in the OC Community

If Red Note’s broader consumer culture helps normalize shopping as emotional comfort, this same logic becomes even more concentrated in smaller niche communities populated by young women. The Red Note tag #稿价分享 (commission price share) reveals a different but equally charged consumer environment. Among female university students active in fandom and illustration circles, OC culture offers not only a hobby but also a form of emotional investment and self-expression. An OC is an original character created by the user. They are often treated not just as a design but as an emotional extension of the self. Under the tag, the visible posts follow a recognizable visual pattern: grids of polished OC illustrations, chat screenshots, payment records, artist names, and captions that foreground how much was spent. Some posts read almost like accounting logs, while others feel closer to proud displays of devotion. Either way, price is made public.

What stands out across these posts is that commission cost is rarely treated as neutral information. Instead, it is a representation of evidence of care, devotion, and taste. Users often emphasize whether the artist is well known, whether the total was especially high, or how long they waited to get a slot. In the comments, other users ask for artist names, react to the total amount, or express admiration. The interaction is not only about finding affordable commissions. It is also about measuring commitment. Within this scene, spending becomes legible as love: the more one pays for an OC, the more one appears to value it.

This idea is reinforced when the viewer moves from Red Note’s tag page to Mihuashi (米画师) itself. This emotional economy is intensified by platform structures. Mihuashi is an art commission platform that centers artist services that make their art styles and commission prices immediately visible. This means the emotional attachment formed around an OC is immediately translated into a highly visible market structure. The jump from admiration to transaction is short. Under these conditions, “famous artists” do not simply charge more because they draw better, but they also accumulate symbolic value because their names raise an OC’s status within the community. The commission price share tag reveals a niche affective economy in which platform visibility, artistic prestige, and emotional devotion are tightly bound together, turning commission spending into both comfort and competition. In short, the commission price share tag reveals a niche affective economy in which platform visibility, artistic prestige, and emotional devotion are tightly bound together, turning commission spending into both comfort and competition.

Discussion

Taken together, these scenes show that emotional consumerism emerges not as an isolated or purely individual behaviour, but as part of a broader platform algorithm in which feelings are continuously interpreted, circulated, and redirected as consumer desire. Across Red Note, algorithmic recommendation systems, comment-based validation, and discourse community cultures all work to bind feeling, visibility, and consumption together. In each case, platforms actively structure consumer behaviours by turning stress, insecurity, attachment, and aspiration into monetizable forms of engagement.

What this paper ultimately reveals is that consumption on digital platforms is increasingly framed not as excess, but as care, care for the self, care for one’s identity, or care for one’s emotional survival. On Red Note, shopping is normalized as comfort and relief; and in the OC commission culture, spending becomes intertwined with love, commitment, and social recognition. These examples demonstrate that emotional consumerism is sustained not only through advertising, but through the ordinary, repeated interactions of scrolling, commenting, saving, comparing, and sharing.
