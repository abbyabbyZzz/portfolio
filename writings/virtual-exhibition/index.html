Rethinking Virtual Exhibitions as Spatial Systems

Exploring how virtual exhibitions transform information into navigable spatial experiences.

In recent years, virtual exhibitions have emerged as a medium to reimagine spatial experience, transforming how audiences interact with artworks and navigate exhibition spaces. This development builds on a longer history of experimentation with the idea of the “virtual museum.” Huhtamo (2010) demonstrates that the idea has circulated for decades across exhibition design, interactive media art, and early digital products. In the early 1990s, for example, “Apple Computer’s ‘Virtual Museum’” offered an interactive 3D simulation navigated by clicking, marking a shift toward viewing the gallery as a computational space (p. 122). Building on this historical trajectory, contemporary virtual exhibitions represent a more immersive stage of development with an emphasis of spatial interaction, user navigation, and participatory engagement.

Rather than simply enhancing existing museum experiences, virtual exhibitions challenge the very distinction between space and information. By reorganizing digital content into navigable spatial systems, they redefine what it means to “visit” an exhibition. This paper argues that such transformations not only expand possibilities for interaction and engagement, but also expose new tensions around access, interface mediation, and the uneven distribution of spatial experience.

Literature Review

Virtual exhibitions are often understood as digital counterparts to physical museums, yet such a view overlooks how they fundamentally reshape the relationship between space, information, and interaction. Virtual exhibitions introduce new ways of structuring and experiencing space. To account for this shift, frameworks such as virtual museum design, augmented space, and hybrid space provide a basis for analyzing how spatial experience is constructed in virtual environments.

Virtual exhibitions as design systems

Virtual exhibitions can be understood as complex design systems that extend the traditional role of museums from simply displaying objects to curating user experience through digital space, interaction, and narrative. As Müller (2010) argues, museums are not only institutions of access but also of interpretation, yet digitization has shift them from “information interpreters to information providers (p. 300).” He proposes seven key design dimensions: space, time, links, storytelling, interactivity, production values, and accessibility, which together form the foundational architecture of virtual exhibitions. These elements transform exhibitions into systems where meaning is not fixed but constructed through navigation, multimedia layering, and user engagement.

Other frameworks further conceptualize virtual museums as systems that integrate content, communication, and participation (Baloian et al., 2021), highlighting their role as structured socio-technical environments. Together, these perspectives emphasize that virtual exhibitions are not merely digital displays but dynamic systems in which curators, designers, and users collectively shape the exhibition experience.

Augmented Space

Manovich (2006) defines augmented space as “the physical space overlaid with dynamically changing information” (p. 219). This concept highlights the relationship between space and information, suggesting that the integration of images, text, and symbolic systems into architecture has long been a central concern. Virtual exhibitions extend this concept further by shifting the relationship between data and space. Instead of functioning as an “ephemeral, immaterial layer over the real space,” dataspace becomes central to the construction of the exhibition itself, effectively turning information into a spatial structure (Manovich, 2006, p. 227). Augmented reality technologies offer new possibilities for integrating data directly into the physical environment, transforming how we engage with art and information in a spatial context.

From this perspective, virtual exhibitions can be understood as an expansion of augmented space. They build on the foundation of spatial practices while pushing it toward fully data-driven environments, in which information does not simply augment space but becomes the medium through which space itself is produced and experienced.

Hybrid space

de Souza e Silva et al. (2025) argue that hybrid spaces emerge from the “blending of physical and digital spaces” through mobility and networked communication (p. 14). They update the concept by emphasizing three dimensions: mobility, connectivity, and sociability. Hybrid space is not only a descriptive label but a theoretical lens for analyzing how networked technologies shape everyday spatial practice.

Virtual exhibitions can be understood as hybrid spaces in which digital and physical spatial logic are connected, reshaping how users experience movement, interaction, and cultural engagement. Rather than existing as purely virtual environments detached from physical reality, virtual exhibitions are produced through the combination of networked technologies, user mobility, and social interaction. At the same time, these spaces are not neutral: access depends on connectivity, devices, and digital literacy, meaning that different users experience different versions of the same exhibition. Therefore, virtual exhibitions are better understood not as isolated digital environments but as socio-technical hybrid spaces, where spatial experience is dynamically constructed through the interplay of technology, mobility, and power.

Case Study of Existing Virtual Exhibition Systems

Case 1: Existing VR Museum Systems

The Museum of Pure Form (MPF) and the Virtual Museum of Sculpture (VMS) are immersive virtual exhibition projects that explore how digital technologies transform the experience of engaging with artworks. MPF addresses the limitations of traditional museums, where interaction is primarily visual, by introducing haptic interfaces that allow users to “touch” digital sculptures, extending access to visually impaired audiences (Carrozzino & Bergamasco, 2010). VMS, in contrast, places digitized sculptures within a navigable virtual environment, where users move through space and access contextual information through text, images, and narration.

From the perspective of augmented and hybrid space, these systems reshape spatial experience in distinct ways. MPF exemplifies augmented space by overlaying digital sensory data onto a physical setting, extending engagement beyond vision through simulated touch. VMS aligns more with hybrid space, where digital and physical spatial logics are integrated into a unified system. In this environment, space is not predetermined but produced through interaction, navigation, and interface design. The audience does not simply stand in front of objects with informational overlays, but they actively construct spatial meaning through movement and engagement.

However, these systems also reveal key limitations of augmented technologies. First, accessibility remains uneven. While MPF enhances tangible access, it depends on specialized hardware and controlled environments, limiting broader participation. Second, the simulation of sensory experience is inherently partial. As Huhtamo (2010) questions, tele-tactility cannot fully replace physical touch, highlighting the mediated nature of virtual interaction. Finally, user experience is structured by interface design, with navigation and interaction constrained by predefined systems. These limitations highlight how virtual exhibition systems simultaneously enable and restrict user experience, reinforcing their nature as socio-technical systems shaped by both design and power.

Case 2: interaction & gamification of virtual gallery

PaintingScapeVR extends beyond content and communication to incorporate collaboration-centric elements through its use of interactivity and gamification, expanding the potential of virtual exhibitions. Cai et al. (2025) argue that protective measures and viewing distance “hinders visitors’ deep appreciation and learning of the artwork,” motivating their attempt to provide “immersive viewing and entertainment” through VR (p. 281). Unlike traditional galleries characterized by passive viewing and limited interaction, this project incorporates treasure-hunting tasks, virtual narrators, and interactive object manipulation, allowing visitors to unlock information about artworks by actively participating in the environment. This design transforms the exhibition from a static display into an experiential system where learning and appreciation are embedded within gameplay.

The project’s design logic aligns with Müller’s (2010) seven-feature framework for online exhibitions, particularly the need for space, storytelling, and interactivity. PaintingScapeVR builds space through explorable 3D environments; it builds storytelling by embedding paintings in scenes that suggest context and meaning; and it builds interactivity by using game-like tasks and feedback loops. The experiment shows a promising finding. Participants demonstrate increased engagement and immersion during gameplay, which contributes to a deeper understanding and appreciation of the artworks (Cai et al., 2025). This demonstrates how VR exhibitions can transform museum visits from passive viewing into active participation (Huhtamo, 2010).

PaintingScapeVR demonstrates a shift from informational layering to experiential immersion. Instead of simply overlaying digital content onto existing space, the system constructs a hybrid environment where spatial navigation, interaction, and meaning making are tightly integrated. Users produce spatial and interpretive meaning through movement and interaction, aligning with the concept of hybrid space as emerging from the mobility of connected users. Gamification further strengthens this process by structuring user movement through goals, feedback, and rewards, effectively turning spatial experience into a guided yet exploratory pathway. As a result, the exhibition space becomes dynamic and responsive, shaped not only by design but also by user agency.

Discussion

Reshaping Augmented Space

While augmented space is traditionally understood as the layering of digital information onto physical environments, virtual exhibitions show a shift toward environments where digital information becomes the primary medium through which space is constructed. In MPF, digital sensory data such as haptic feedback enhances physical interaction, extending the logic of augmented space beyond visual overlays. In VMS and PaintingScapeVR, space is no longer augmented but produced through digital structures, navigation systems, and interaction design. In this sense, virtual exhibitions move from augmenting space to constructing space, where information, interface, and user movement function as the architecture of the exhibition experience.

Transformation of Spatial Experience

Across cases, the role of the audience shifts from passive observer to active participant, with meaning constructed through movement, interaction, and engagement. These systems align with the concept of hybrid space, where spatial experience emerges from the mobility of connected users. This transformation enhances engagement and immersion, suggesting that virtual exhibitions can offer more dynamic and participatory forms of cultural experience compared to traditional museum settings.

Limitations

The case studies also reveal significant limitations that complicate the potential of virtual exhibitions. First, access remains uneven. Systems such as MPF require specialized hardware and technical expertise, while even more accessible platforms depend on stable internet connectivity and digital literacy. Second, while gamification enhances immersion, it risks shifting exhibitions toward entertainment-driven experiences, potentially undermining cultural depth. These factors reveal that virtual exhibitions not only transform space but also reorganize access, control, and user experience in uneven ways.

Conclusion

This paper argues that virtual exhibitions transform augmented space by shifting digital information from a supplementary layer into a spatial system structured through interaction, navigation, and user engagement. In doing so, they reorganize information into space itself, turning museum visiting into a form of navigational practice rather than passive viewing.

The case studies demonstrate that virtual exhibitions offer strong potential for enhancing engagement, accessibility, and new forms of cultural experience. However, as hybrid spaces, they are also shaped by uneven access to technologies, interface limitations, and the risk of sensory overload or excessive gamification. These challenges highlight that their future development depends not only on technological advancement but also on thoughtful design, curatorial strategies, and broader social considerations.

This paper argues that virtual exhibitions transform augmented space by shifting digital information from a supplementary layer into a spatial system structured through interaction, navigation, and user engagement. In doing so, they reorganize information into space itself, turning museum visiting into a form of navigational practice rather than passive viewing.

Ultimately, virtual exhibitions can be understood as dynamic socio-technical systems that enrich augmented space. Future development will require balancing immersion with accessibility and interactivity with interpretation, as they increasingly redefine how cultural spaces are produced and experienced in the digital age.
