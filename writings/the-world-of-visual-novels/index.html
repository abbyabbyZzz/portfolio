Paths of Memory and Discovery: The World of Visual Novels

Examines 10 Japanese visual novels from year 1997 to 2007 crossing animation, gaming, and interactive fiction from their interactivity and branching narratives that illustrate personal identity, memory, and emotional growth.

Introduction

Visual novels represent a unique combination of interactive storytelling and anime-styled digital art, standing at the crossroads of anime, gaming, and literature. Unlike traditional video games, which often emphasize action and reflex-driven gameplay, visual novels prioritize narrative components, immersing players in the rich storylines shaped by their own choices. These interactive experiences employ branching narratives, where player decisions determine the direction of the plot, leading to multiple possible outcomes. This non-linear approach differentiates visual novels from conventional storytelling forms, offering a complex relation between interactivity and narration. By weaving together text, character portraits, background art, and some animation, visual novels create immersive emotionally resonant worlds that have influenced both gaming and anime industries.

Different from anime, visual novels adapted a multiperspectival nature, incorporating multiple timelines, alternative realities, or fragmented recollections to enhance player engagement as well as experience. Themes of memory, trauma, and self-discovery journey often dominate the narratives of visual novels, reflecting the emotional depth of both the protagonists and the player. Moreover, these stories blend magical and mundane elements, mixing everyday life with supernatural or psychological components. Through delicately constructed aesthetics—detailed backgrounds, expressive character designs, and evocative soundtracks—visual novels bring their player into deeply personal, often melancholic journeys of gameplay.

The central theme connecting the artifacts of this exhibition is the journey of self-discovery through memories and personal choices. Returning to the past as well as journeys of self-discovery have been a recurring theme of Japanese visual novels. This can be exemplified in narratives where protagonists return to childhood homes, rekindle old relationships, or recover lost memories. At the same time, these stories resist linear progression, encouraging audiences to put up fragments of narration by themselves. In this sense, the player engages with visual novels in an active, interpretative manner.

This journal showcases the evolution of these themes across 10 influential visual novels. Beginning with To Heart (1997) and White Album (1998) toward later works like Myself; Yourself (2007), this exhibition invites viewers to experience firsthand the defining feature of visual novels: the power of choice. Each branching route, each moment of decision, echoes a central truth—that our identities are fluid, shaped by both our past experiences and our willingness to explore new possibilities. By intertwining player agency with rich narratives of memory and identity, these visual novels offer a unique interactive storytelling experience that challenges traditional notions of linear storytelling and encourages deeper engagement with the characters and themes presented.

to heart (1997)

To Heart (originally published in 1997 and 1999 for different platforms) lied the foundation for the narrative of visual novels by experimenting with tropes that would become central to the visual novel medium. The game also established the journey of self-discovery as one of visual novel’s most prevalent themes. In the game, this discovery of self-identity is unraveled from the start through a flashback to Akari’s, the female protagonist, and Hiroyuki’s, the male protagonist, childhood. Throughout the story of To Heart, Akari’s idealistic conviction is getting complicated multiple times, which heavily contributes to her growth and “self-exploratory trajectory” (Cavallaro, 2010, p. 201). Moreover, Hiroyuki stands as the “unorthodox” (Cavallaro, 2010, p. 201) protagonist within a branching narrative, entangled with multiple female characters whose relationships with him unfold through various motivations and not always following straightforward logic. Nevertheless, To Heart handled its narration with endearing sensitivity to the real feelings flowing between each character. In To Heart, player choice serves as the narrative engine that transforms the protagonist’s journey from a passive story into an interactive experience. Each choice is not merely codes to different endings but reflections of Hiroyuki’s evolving priorities and vulnerabilities, mirroring the nonlinear process of self-discovery. The game’s emphasis on choice underscores its thematic focus on agency and consequence. Each route functions as an exploration of identity, where Hiroyuki’s decisions—in other words, the player’s—reveal how relationships shape self-perception. For instance, a choice to confront Akari’s idealized view of their childhood forces her to reconcile fantasy with reality, catalyzing her growth. To Heart pioneers a narrative model where choice is not just a mechanic but a metaphor for the iterative process of understanding oneself and others, which influences many future visual novels.

White Album (1998)

White Album is a 1998 visual novel developed by Leaf, notable for its innovative gameplay mechanics and introspective narrative structure that set it apart from other romance visual novels of its time. Unlike To Heart that focus on branching romantic developments, White Album presents a psychological exploration of love, ambition, and personal sacrifice, centering on the protagonist’s evolving relationship within a specific historical and social context—Japan 1986.

Expanding on the mechanics of choices in traditional visual novels, a unique gameplay feature of White Album is its schedule management system, which requires players to plan the protagonist’s weekly activities. This mechanic influences character interactions and event triggers, reinforcing the importance of player choice that encourages multiple playthroughs. The ability to choose different schedules affects how the protagonist engages with the heroines, allowing players to explore various emotional arcs, conflicts, and personal dilemmas. Conversations are also interactive, with players selecting dialogue topics, further shaping narrative direction.

Different from To Heart, White Album depicts a “multifaceted and flawed” male protagonist where his long-term good intentions are often “perverted by the yearning for immediate satisfaction” (Cavallaro, 2010, p. 239). This in-depth characterization contrasts with the more straightforward archetypes of early visual novels, making White Album a pioneering work depicting the protagonist not as an idealized romantic lead but as a flawed individual navigating emotional uncertainty.

Visually, White Album embraces a minimalist aesthetic, employing watercolour-style backgrounds and simple animation effects to emphasize mood and atmosphere rather than dynamic action. This presentation complements the game’s themes of melancholy, nostalgia, and quiet emotional struggles, reinforcing its psychological depth over conventional visual novels. By blending a historically grounded setting with an intimate narrative about ambition, love, and disillusionment, White Album stands as a seminal work in the visual novel genre, influencing later titles that sought to blend realism with emotional complexity.

Kanon (1999)

The Kanon game (1999) started with the return of its protagonist, Yuuichi Aizawa, to a town he has not visited in seven years. The visual novel depicted a story that connects memory, loss, and the passage of time with a narrative that shifts between the mundane and the supernatural. As one of the significant works of the genre, Kanon expands on the theme of self-discovery through memories and personal choices. This journey into the past becomes a central narrative device, where forgotten relationships, childhood promises, and long-buried emotions gradually resurface, shaping both the protagonist and the player’s understanding of the story.

The visual novel features a branching narrative structure, allowing the player to interact with multiple female characters, each with her own mysterious connection to Yuuichi’s past. Each route explores a different emotional landscape, from lighthearted romance to deep tragedy, emphasizing the unbreakable ties between memory and identity. Amnesia, a recurring motif, reflects the fragility of human connections and the inevitable distortions of time, emphasizing the idea that only confronting the past can make one move forward.

The visual of Kanon is characterized by its soft colour palette, dreamlike backgrounds, and expressive character designs, all of which enhance its melancholic and nostalgic atmosphere. By blending romance, tragedy, and fantasy, Kanon set a precedent for later visual novels, proving that the genre could be both interactive and profoundly deep.

Rumbling Hearts (2001)

Rumbling Hearts (Kimi ga Nozomu Eien, 2001) developed by age, is a deeply emotional visual novel that examines the transition from juvenile expectations to the realities of adulthood. Rumbling Hearts focuses on a narrative of love, loss, and guilt, where past and present collide, forcing characters to choose between painful dilemmas.

The story follows Takayuki Narumi, a young man caught in an emotional chaos after his loved childhood friend, Haruka Suzumiya, falls into a three-year coma following a car accident. As time moves forward, Takayuki finds himself in a relationship with Mitsuki Hayase, Haruka’s best friend, leading to a profound guilt when Haruka awakens—unaware of the years that have passed. The game presents players with difficult choices that alter the trajectory of relationships, emphasizing how time and personal responsibility shape identity and emotional well-being.

Central to the game’s narrative is its use of flashbacks and psychological fragmentation, as Takayuki’s conflicting emotions exacerbate, revealing the deep scars left by time and trauma. Unlike traditional branching romance stories, where players build relationships from a neutral starting point, Rumbling Hearts forces them to reconcile past memories with present realities, making each decision feel deeply consequential.

Visually, Rumbling Hearts employs muted tones and expressive character designs to heighten the emotional gravity of its story. Its melancholic soundtrack further reinforces the themes of nostalgia, regret, and the irreversible passage of time. By presenting a multi-perspective exploration of love and personal sacrifice, Rumbling Hearts exemplifies the journey of self-discovery through memories and choices, making it a landmark visual novel in emotional storytelling.

clannad (2004)

Clannad (2004), developed by Key, explores family, memory, and coming of age through its dual-arc structure: School Life and After Story. Its unique feature of dual arc helps extending Clannad’s story beyond high school relationships to depict the realities of adulthood, making it one of the most narratively rich visual novels of its time. The School Life arc follows Tomoya Okazaki, a disillusioned high school senior, as he interacts with five central heroines—Nagisa Furukawa, Kyou Fujibayashi, Tomoyo Sakagami, Kotomi Ichinose, and Fuko Ibuki. Each heroine’s route delves into personal struggles, hidden pasts, and self-discovery, emphasizing the importance of connections and resilience. Upon completing these arcs, players unlock After Story, which shifts the focus to Tomoya and Nagisa’s life after graduation, tackling themes of parenthood, hardship, and the enduring power of love.

Similar to Kanon, Clannad focuses on capitalizing on the narrative principle of multiperspectivalism where seemingly independent storylines interweave into a larger, interconnected experience by creating a series of interrelated stories and respective journeys of self-discovery. This mechanic reinforces the idea that understanding others’ perspectives is essential for personal growth. Moreover, this narrative structure creates a literary depth in the atmosphere of the game, “absorbing” the player into the game’s storyline, encouraging the player to reflect on their own reality (Cavallaro, 2010, p. 229). This makes Clannad successful in both the realm of game and the realm of anime with its anime adaptation. We as players are encouraged to consider how the characters' experiences relate to our own lives, prompting us to examine the underlying ideological assumptions that subtly shape our personal journeys of self-discovery.

School Days (2005)

School Days (2005) portrays an unconventional journey of self-discovery in the field of visual novels, where it unravels a dark and disturbing narrative of deception, obsession, and emotional collapse. The story centers on depicting a twisted psychological drama, where selfish desires and moral failures lead to catastrophic consequences. The game follows Makoto Itou, a high school student who, rather than forming meaningful connections, degenerates himself to an impulsive path of seduction, entangling himself with multiple heroines. His actions, fueled by immaturity and uncontrolled desire, expose the darker side of human relationships. The choices players make determine whether the narrative unfolds as a tragic downfall or a complete psychological breakdown, cumulating to a series of bloody and unsettling endings.

Different from other traditional visual novels that emphasize character growth and redemption, School Days presents a grotesque and flawed journey of self-discovery, where the protagonists fall into moral corruption instead of personal enlightenment. By dissecting the darker part of the human mind and its destructive consequences, School Days stands as one of the most unsettling visual novels, offering an uncompromising look at the darker facets of love and desire. It is difficult to imagine a journey of self-discovery more “brutally yet awesomely flawed” (Cavallaro, 2010, p. 239) than the one depicted in School Days.

Kashimashi—Girl Meets Girl (2006)

Kashimashi—Girl Meets Girl based on the original manga, presents a unique journey of self-discovery through identity transformation and emotional reevaluation. Different from former visual novels, Kashimashi challenges gender identity, self-perception, and the evolving nature of love. The story follows Hazumu Osaragi, a high school boy who got fatally injured in an alien-spacecraft accident. The aliens successfully bring him back to life, but Hazumu now becomes biologically female. This sudden change forces Hazumu to reconsider personal identity, relationships, and emotions that go beyond typical visual novel narratives. The game blends mundane and extraordinary in order to explore the real nature of human experience of love, belonging, and self-acceptance. On the way Hazumu is accepting their new identity, the story delves into fluidity in attraction, the redefinition of friendships, and the tension between past and present self-conceptions. The player must make choices that determine how Hazumu adapts to life after this transformation, shaping interactions with close friends.

Unlike many visual novels that use romance as an end goal, Kashimashi utilizes it as a vehicle for self-discovery, highlighting how attraction, affection, and identity are fluid and ever-changing. The game does not offer absolute answers, nor does it demand that Hazumu—or the player—adhere to rigid expectations. Instead, it celebrates transformation in all its uncertainty, proving that the search for identity is not about reaching a conclusion, but about embracing the process itself.

True tears (2006)

True Tears (2006) examines self-discovery with emotional ambiguity through its intricate, multi-perspective storytelling. What is special about True Tears is that it immerses the player in a web of misrecognitions, emotional wounds, and layered psychological struggles, creating a sensitive and dramatic narrative tapestry.

At the heart of the story is Shinichirou Nakagami, an aspiring artist whos relationships with Hiromi Yuasa and Noe Isurugi reflect the tension between the past and the present, familial responses, and unspoken desires. The Visual novel’s structure is divided into weekly segments, allowing the player to select specific days, triggering different events that shape the course of the story. First, this function raises the player’s agency in shaping Shinichirou’s journey, and second, the function demonstrates how small decisions may lead to unexpected outcomes.

The game places a special focus on the emotional scars hidden beneath the surface of familial and pseudo-familial relationships. The past is revisited not as distant memory but as an active stage where characters struggle with contradictory desires and suppressed emotions. This intertwine of various personal experiences creates a deep narrative, pulling the player into an interactive and immersive process.

Another unique narrative element in True Tears is Shinichirou’s picture book, showing the story of the chickens Raigomaru and Jibeta. This serves as an allegorical len towards Shinichirou’s inner struggle and his relationships with Hiromi as well as Noe. This artistic style of his paintings occasionally appears into the main narrative, reinforcing the game’s dreamlike, magical atmosphere. By mixing deeply psychological storytelling with visually expressive aesthetic, True Tears presents a profoundly emotional experience. Its story exhibits emotional depth, making the journey of self-discovery as compelling as it is uncertain.

H2O: Footprints in the Sand (2006)

H2O: Footprints in the Sand (2006), developed by Makura, explores perception, trauma, and reconciliation with the past. It stands out from other visual novels with its dual gameplay modes, which mirror the protagonist’s journey from blindness—both physical and psychological-- to clarity and acceptance.

The story follows Takuma Hirose, a visually impaired boy who returns to his hometown, Sawai, attempting to recover from an unspecified illness. As the story progresses and forms relationships with the key heroines, he is forced to confront painful buried memories and the dark, oppressive, traditions of the town. His personal healing is linked to the emotional scars around him, resulting from societal cruelty and familiar loss. A unique mechanic of H2O is its two distinct gameplay modes, each affecting the game’s visual and narrative presentation. The Blindness Mode presents the world in a monochromatic, dreamlike palette, reflecting Takuma’s impaired vision and metaphorically emphasizing his detachment from reality. As he slowly regains his sight, the game’s colours return, symbolizing his psychological awakening. The Normal Mode maintains full colour from the beginning, allowing the player to experience the story without chromatic distortion. The player can switch between these modes even within a single playthrough, reinforcing the theme of shifting perception in the journey of self-discovery—how truth can be obscured by personal trauma and gradually revealed.

H2O diverges from traditional visual novels by lacing text across the entire screen in a transparent box, further emphasizing the importance of narration and internal reflection over external interaction. This feature immerses the player in the protagonist’s fragmented psyche, making them actively participating in his journey of discovery. The “return-to-the-past” motif reaches its emotional peak when Takuma is forced to confront his repressed memories of his mother’s suicide. This revelation forces Takuma to acknowledge the pain he has long sought to escape, highlighting the belief that healing can only come through accepting.

Myself; Yourself (2007)

Like many visual novels introduced formerly in the exhibition, Myself; Yourself (2007) depicts a journey to self that explores memory and the unsettling nature of revisiting the past. Like Kanon, the game evokes a strange and ambiguous feeling. This duality of comforting nostalgia and darkness drives the narrative, compelling both the protagonist and the player to unravel buried truths hidden beneath the town’s peaceful surface. The story starts with Sana Hidaka, a high school student who returns to his hometown to reconnect with his childhood friends. However, he finds the town subtly changed with an unspoken tension.

A defining strength of Myself; Yourself is its masterful use of ambiguity. The game unfolds its secrets gradually, weaving a web of misdirection that calls on an active player participation. The player must piece together fragmented clues to uncover hidden layers of meaning.

The game also masters its visuals to fit the atmosphere. It utilizes a poetic depiction of setting, using richly detailed backgrounds and atmospheric lighting to enhance emotional weight of each scene. The soundtrack further amplifies the tension, seamlessly transitioning between melancholic piano pieces and unsettling ambient tones, reinforcing the delicate balance between sentimentality and dread.

In short, Myself; Yourself is not just a story about returning home, but about rediscovering oneself through the memories that shape us. The game challenges players to engage with its narrative on a deeper level, rewarding those who look beyond the surface with a focus on loss, identity, and the irreversible passage of time.
