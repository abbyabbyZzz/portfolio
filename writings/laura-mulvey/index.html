Visual Pleasure and Narrative Cinema Article Analysis

Visual Pleasure and Narrative Cinema, written by Laura Mulvey, emerged as an important piece within film theory, analyzing the discourse on gender, spectatorship, and power dynamics within the realm of Hollywood cinema. In the article, Mulvey navigates through the visual pleasure of cinema and how the power dynamics between different genders play a role in constructing woman images in cinematic narratives. Mulvey introduces two dichotomic concepts that provide the audience with the pleasure of looking: scopophilia and ego libido. She theorizes that the audience may gain pleasure from either actively objectifying an unknowing other or forgetting themselves and identifying with the ideal ego onscreen. The idea remains relevant in the context of classical Hollywood cinema. However, the audience engages with visual media not only through traditional cinema but also through various digital platforms, and Mulvey’s theory on woman image could therefore be transformed.

The article introduces two origins of visual pleasure within cinema. The first one s scopophilia, originally introduced by Freud, the pleasure of viewing other people as objects and subjecting them to a controlling and curious gaze. This concept can be applied to cinemas where the audience gains pleasure through in the cinema where a “sealed world” is unravelled and presented on screen with strong lights in a dark auditorium3. This setting of cinema brings the audience with a sense of voyeurism, which helps them exert power as well as gaze toward the characters within the screen. Mulvey then analyzes how females in Hollywood movies are designed to be passive, and their appearance is coded as an erotic object for both characters within the screen and for the audience within the auditorium4. Pleasure can also originate from narcissistic identification, where the audience forgets about themselves and adopts an ideal ego from the characters, mostly the protagonist, within the screen. The character with such a feature is often the male protagonist in a film, with a perfect, powerful, and three-dimensional ideal ego presented to the audience5. The setting of cinema creates a sense of voyeurism and power, while female characters are often passive and erotic objects.

Visual culture in contemporary society is constructed by multiple media creating an image-saturated world. Even though the medium shifts from a single-channel broadcast like cinemas to a more participatory culture where consumers can join in the social production, Mulvey’s theory can be applied to many of the modern visual media today. In today’s context, females are still mostly depicted as passive and are the objects being watched. For instance, in many webcam platforms, female users are often exposing themselves to other users through screens. They are aware of the gaze, or surveillance, from other people, which frames their self-representations and shapes their identity formation. This surveillance culture also increases self-sexualization among adolescent girls, where they feel pressured to conform to the erotic image of female figures. Mulvey’s theory continues to be relevant in understanding how women are portrayed and consumed in visual media, perpetuating gender stereotypes and inequalities. It is important for society to critically analyze and challenge these representations to promote more diverse and empowering depictions of women.

Laura Mulvey's theory of visual pleasure and gender dynamics in narrative cinema in the article Visual Pleasure and Narrative Cinema remains relevant in today's image-saturated world, despite shifts in media consumption habits and technologies. The passive and erotic portrayal of women figures persists throughouttime, perpetuating gender stereotypes and inequalities.
